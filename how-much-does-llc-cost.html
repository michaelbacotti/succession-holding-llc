How Much Does an LLC Cost? Full Breakdown by State

Everything you need to budget for when forming and maintaining a Limited Liability Company — one-time costs and ongoing expenses.

Forming a Limited Liability Company is one of the most common steps a real estate investor takes to protect personal assets and optimize tax treatment. But many first-time LLC owners are surprised by the total cost picture — which extends well beyond the initial filing fee. Understanding all the costs involved, before you file, helps you budget correctly and avoid unexpected bills in year two.

This guide breaks down every cost category associated with forming and maintaining an LLC, from the one-time filing fee to ongoing state obligations. We also provide a state-by-state cost comparison for five commonly-used states so you can see where the real expenses lie.

One-Time LLC Costs

Filing Fee

The Articles of Organization filing fee is the most visible upfront cost. Every state charges a fee to process the formation documents — currently ranging from around $50 to $800 or more, depending on the state. This is a one-time fee paid at formation. Some states also charge an additional franchise tax or publication fee at the time of filing.

Registered Agent Cost

Every LLC must maintain a registered agent — a person or service designated to receive legal documents on the LLC's behalf. You can serve as your own registered agent at no cost, or hire a commercial registered agent service for $0 to $300 per year. Many investors use a commercial service for privacy and convenience, since the registered agent's name and address typically appear in public records.

Operating Agreement

The operating agreement is the governing document that establishes the LLC's internal rules — ownership percentages, profit distribution, management structure, and procedures for adding or removing members. You can draft a basic operating agreement yourself using a template (free to $100), or hire an attorney for a more customized agreement ($500 to $2,000+). For a single-member LLC with straightforward terms, a DIY template is often sufficient.

EIN Registration

An Employer Identification Number is free from the IRS. You obtain it online in minutes at no cost. Some formation services charge $50–$75 to obtain an EIN on your behalf, but there is no reason to pay for this — the IRS provides it directly at irs.gov at no charge.

Ongoing LLC Costs

Annual Report Fees

Most states require LLCs to file an annual report and pay a corresponding fee. Annual report fees typically range from $25 to $800 depending on the state. Some states calculate the fee based on the LLC's revenue or par value of assets, which can make the cost unpredictable from year to year.

Franchise Taxes

Several states — particularly Delaware, Texas, and California — charge an annual franchise tax on LLCs operating in or registered with the state. Delaware's franchise tax is based on authorized shares and can range from $175 to $300 per year. Texas imposes a 0.375% franchise tax on margin for qualifying LLCs. California charges an $800 annual LLC tax minimum. If you form your LLC in one of these states but operate elsewhere, you still need to understand these ongoing obligations.

Registered Agent Renewal

If you use a commercial registered agent service, you will pay an annual renewal fee — typically $100 to $300 per year. Some services offer multi-year discounts.

Registered Agent vs. Foreign Qualification

If your LLC is formed in one state but you conduct business in another, you may need to register as a foreign LLC in that state — with its own filing fee and annual obligations. Budget for this if you invest in multiple states.

One-Time vs. Ongoing: What to Budget

When planning your LLC budget, separate one-time formation costs from recurring annual expenses. A typical first-year budget for an LLC in a low-cost state like Wyoming — using a commercial registered agent — might look like this:

  • Formation filing fee: $100
  • Registered agent (first year): $100
  • Operating agreement (DIY template): $0–$50
  • EIN registration: $0
  • Annual report: $52
  • Total first year: $252–$302

By contrast, forming in Delaware or Nevada — where many investors are drawn by favorable corporate law — can cost $500–$600 in year one before considering franchise taxes.

Is Forming in a Low-Cost State Always Better?

Not necessarily. While Wyoming and Nevada have low formation costs and strong privacy protections, the right state for your LLC depends on where you actually conduct business, what type of real estate you are investing in, and whether you need access to a particular state's courts for dispute resolution. Many investors choose to form the LLC in the state where the property is located to avoid foreign qualification fees and simplify compliance. Forming in Wyoming and then qualifying to do business in Texas, for example, means paying filing fees in both states.

Key Takeaway

The total cost of an LLC goes well beyond the initial filing fee. First-year costs typically range from $150 to $600 depending on state, registered agent choices, and attorney involvement. Ongoing annual costs range from $50 to $800+ depending on the state's franchise tax and annual report requirements. Budget for both when planning your real estate investment structure.
