Seller Financing for Real Estate: How It Works and When to Use It

Seller financing lets buyers and sellers transact without a bank. Here's how to structure a deal that works for both sides.

Last updated: April 2026 • By the Succession Holding LLC Research Team

Not every real estate transaction requires a bank. In seller financing — also called owner financing — the property seller extends credit directly to the buyer, taking back a promissory note secured by the property itself. The buyer makes a down payment and pays the seller directly in monthly installments, with interest, according to agreed-upon terms. For the right property and the right buyer, seller financing eliminates the delays, requirements, and costs associated with traditional mortgage financing.

How Seller Financing Works

In a seller-financed transaction, the parties execute two core documents:

  • The Promissory Note: A written promise by the buyer to repay the seller the financed amount, plus agreed-upon interest, according to a specified payment schedule. The note specifies the principal balance, interest rate, monthly payment amount, amortization term, maturity date, and consequences of default.
  • The Deed of Trust or Mortgage: The security instrument that gives the seller (now the lender) a lien against the property. This lien is recorded in the county land records and protects the seller if the buyer fails to make payments. If the buyer defaults, the seller can foreclose to recover the property.

In some states, the security instrument is called a mortgage; in others, it's a deed of trust. Either way, it serves the same function — it gives the seller a legal claim against the property that can be enforced through foreclosure if necessary.

Advantages for Buyers

Seller financing offers several distinct advantages over conventional bank financing:

  • No lender underwriting. There is no bank evaluating your credit score, debt-to-income ratio, or employment history. The seller is extending credit based on their own assessment of the buyer and the property. This makes seller financing accessible to buyers who might not qualify for conventional financing — including self-employed individuals with complex income situations, recent immigrants, or investors using an LLC entity that doesn't fit conventional lending criteria.
  • No loan limits. Conventional mortgages are subject to conforming loan limits set by FHFA ($766,550 for single-family homes in most markets in 2024). Seller financing can cover any purchase price, making it particularly useful for higher-value properties or commercial transactions.
  • Flexible terms. The parties can negotiate any terms they agree on — interest rate, amortization schedule, balloon payment date, prepayment privileges. This flexibility lets creative buyers and sellers structure deals that work for their specific circumstances.
  • Faster closing. Without a lender's underwriting, appraisal contingency, and loan approval process, closing timelines compress significantly. A seller-financed transaction can often close in two to three weeks from accepted offer, compared to 30 to 45 days for a conventional mortgage.

Advantages for Sellers

Sellers benefit from seller financing as well, particularly in the right market conditions:

  • Higher sale price and better terms. A seller willing to finance can often command a premium on the sale price, because they're offering something valuable — the ability to close quickly, without bank contingencies, with flexible terms. Studies of seller-financed transactions consistently show higher effective sale prices compared to conventional transactions.
  • Steady income stream. Instead of receiving a lump sum at closing and investing it elsewhere, the seller receives monthly payments over time — providing retirement income, cash flow, or a diversified return profile. For sellers who are motivated by income rather than liquidity, this can be a meaningful advantage.
  • Interest income. The interest portion of each payment is taxable income to the seller, often at favorable capital gains rates or as ordinary income depending on the structure and the seller's overall tax situation.
  • Security. The deed of trust lien gives the seller the right to foreclose if the buyer defaults — so the seller retains a strong legal remedy if the arrangement goes wrong. The risk is real, but it's not unsecured risk.

Typical Seller Financing Terms

While seller-financed deals are highly negotiable, there are norms that most transactions follow:

  • Down payment: Typically 10% to 30% of the purchase price. Larger down payments reduce the seller's risk and signal buyer commitment. A 20% to 25% down payment is common on residential investment property.
  • Interest rate: Usually slightly above prevailing conventional mortgage rates, since the seller is taking on more risk than a bank. Rate spreads of 1% to 3% above conventional rates are common, depending on the buyer's creditworthiness and the property condition.
  • Amortization schedule: The period over which the loan is paid down. 15-year and 30-year amortization are common. A shorter amortization means higher monthly payments but faster equity build-up for the buyer and faster payoff for the seller.
  • Balloon payment: Many seller-financed transactions include a balloon payment — a large lump sum due at the end of a specified term (often 3 to 5 years), with the buyer required to refinance the balance through a conventional lender before the balloon comes due. This protects the seller from long-term credit risk and gives the buyer a path to conventional financing once they've established a payment history and built equity.

Due Diligence on the Seller

Before entering a seller-financing arrangement, buyers should investigate the seller's right to sell and the status of any existing financing on the property. Specifically:

  • Existing mortgage payoff: If the seller has an existing mortgage, the proceeds from the sale must pay it off. Confirm the approximate payoff amount and ensure the seller's existing loan allows for a due-on-sale transfer — most conventional mortgages contain this clause, which requires full payoff upon sale. An existing loan that cannot be paid off at closing creates significant complications.
  • Clear title: Order a title search before closing. You need to confirm that the seller actually owns the property free and clear of liens, judgments, or other encumbrances that would cloud your title.
  • Seller's motivation: Understanding why a seller is offering financing is important. A seller who needs the monthly income is a very different counterparty than one who cannot sell conventionally because of condition or marketability issues.

When Seller Financing Makes Sense

Seller financing is not appropriate for every transaction, but it creates genuine advantages in specific situations:

  • Investment property acquisitions: Many investment property buyers use LLCs or other entities that don't qualify for conventional mortgage financing. Seller financing lets them acquire property that they couldn't otherwise finance.
  • Distressed or unique properties: Properties in poor condition, non-conforming parcels, or unusual configurations often don't qualify for conventional appraisals. A seller willing to finance bypasses the appraisal requirement entirely.
  • Hot markets with fast appreciation: In rapidly appreciating markets, buyers willing to offer seller financing can sometimes acquire properties that would otherwise receive multiple conventional offers, by providing the seller with a premium price and favorable terms.
  • Seller liquidity needs: A seller who wants to defer capital gains (through an installment sale structure) or who prefers monthly income over a lump sum may actively seek seller-financing buyers.

The Bottom Line

Seller financing is a tool — useful in the right situation, unnecessary in others. The key is understanding what you're agreeing to on both sides of the transaction. If you're buying with seller financing, get a promissory note reviewed by a real estate attorney before you sign. If you're selling with seller financing, require the same from your buyer and make sure your deed of trust is properly recorded in the county land records. Done right, seller financing creates a win for both parties. Done carelessly, it creates legal and financial exposure that neither side wants.
