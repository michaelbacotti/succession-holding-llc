LLC vs. Sole Proprietorship

Understanding the key differences between operating as a sole proprietor and forming a Limited Liability Company — and which structure is right for you.

Last updated: April 2026 • By the Succession Holding LLC Research Team

One of the first decisions any new business owner or real estate investor faces is choosing a legal structure for their operations. Two of the most common options — the sole proprietorship and the Limited Liability Company — sit at opposite ends of the spectrum in terms of formality, cost, liability protection, and tax treatment. Understanding the real-world differences between them is essential before you commit to either path.

What Is a Sole Proprietorship?

A sole proprietorship is the simplest business structure available. There is no formal registration requirement — you are already running one any time you conduct business under your own name, or under a name that is simply your personal name. You report business income and expenses on a Schedule C attached to your personal tax return, and there is no legal separation between you and the business.

This simplicity is the sole proprietorship's greatest advantage. There are no formation fees, no annual report filings, and no formalities to maintain. The downside, however, is significant: as a sole proprietor, you bear personal liability for every business decision, contract, and lawsuit. If a tenant sues you over an injury that occurred on your rental property, your personal assets — your home, savings, and investments — are all on the line.

What Is an LLC?

A Limited Liability Company is a business entity that separates your personal assets from your business liabilities. To form an LLC, you file articles of organization with your state's Secretary of State and pay a filing fee, which varies by state but is typically between $50 and $500. Most LLCs also adopt an operating agreement — an internal document outlining how the company is run, even if there is only one member.

The core benefit of an LLC is the liability shield. If the business is sued or takes on debt, the owner's personal assets are generally protected. This is a meaningful advantage for any activity that carries inherent risk — including real estate investment, where property damage, tenant injuries, and contract disputes are routine occurrences.

Liability Protection: The Core Difference

Liability protection is where the two structures diverge most dramatically. As a sole proprietor, your personal wealth is exposed. If a tenant slips on ice at your rental property and sues, a judgment against the business is a judgment against you personally. Your personal assets can be seized to satisfy business debts and legal judgments.

An LLC creates a legal firewall. The LLC itself — not you personally — owns the property and enters into contracts. A lawsuit naming the LLC targets the LLC's assets, not yours. This separation is not absolute — courts can "pierce the corporate veil" in cases of fraud, co-mingling of funds, or serious undercapitalization — but a properly maintained LLC provides strong, legitimate protection for the vast majority of situations.

For anyone holding real estate as an investment, the liability protection alone is often sufficient justification to form an LLC. The property itself is often the most valuable asset a person owns, and shielding it from lawsuits is a prudent risk management step.

Tax Implications

Both sole proprietorships and single-member LLCs are "pass-through" entities for federal tax purposes. This means the business itself does not pay income tax. Instead, all profits pass through to the owner's personal tax return, where they are taxed at ordinary income rates.

A sole proprietor reports on Schedule C. A single-member LLC also reports on Schedule C by default — the IRS treats the LLC as a disregarded entity for tax purposes unless you elect to be taxed as a corporation. This means the day-to-day tax filing for a solo LLC is essentially identical to that of a sole proprietorship.

However, an LLC can elect to be taxed as an S-Corporation or C-Corporation, which may offer tax savings in certain circumstances. An S-Corp election allows you to pay yourself a reasonable salary and take additional distributions as non-wage income, potentially reducing self-employment tax. A C-Corp election opens the door to corporate-level tax planning, including retained earnings and qualified small business stock exclusions, though it also triggers double taxation on profits distributed to owners.

These elections involve tradeoffs and complexity. For most real estate investors operating as a sole member LLC, the default pass-through treatment is appropriate unless a tax professional advises otherwise based on your specific income level and situation.

Paperwork and Ongoing Requirements

A sole proprietorship requires almost no paperwork beyond your standard personal tax return. The only potential compliance step is registering a dba ("doing business as") name if you operate under something other than your legal name — and even that varies by jurisdiction.

An LLC requires more upfront effort. You must file formation documents, pay a filing fee, and — in most states — publish a notice of formation in a local newspaper for a period of weeks. Annually, most states require a registered agent and an annual report filing, typically with a fee between $50 and $800 depending on the state. Some states also impose franchise taxes or minimum fees on LLCs regardless of income.

Beyond state fees, an LLC benefits from maintaining its own bank account, keeping separate accounting records, and documenting major decisions in meeting minutes or written consents. These steps reinforce the liability shield and ensure the LLC is treated as a genuine separate entity should it ever be challenged in litigation.

When a Sole Proprietorship Makes Sense

Sole proprietorships are most appropriate for low-risk activities where the potential for lawsuits or significant liability is minimal. A freelance writer, consultant, or small-scale crafter operating from home with limited client exposure may find the simplicity of a sole proprietorship perfectly adequate. The cost and paperwork savings are real, and the liability exposure may be manageable given the nature of the work.

However, even low-risk businesses often carry more exposure than their owners realize. A client who trips over equipment, a product that causes damage, or a contract dispute can create liability that reaches into personal assets regardless of how modest the business appears.

When an LLC Makes Sense

An LLC is the default recommendation for anyone operating a real estate investment business. The liability protection is directly applicable — rental properties carry meaningful risk exposure from tenants, contractors, and the public. Separating your personal assets from your investment operations through an LLC is a fundamental risk management practice used by virtually every serious real estate investor.

An LLC also provides a cleaner structure for bringing in partners, selling interests, or transferring assets. Ownership stakes in an LLC are clearly defined, and the entity can continue to exist independently of any individual owner, providing continuity that a sole proprietorship cannot offer.

The additional cost and paperwork are modest relative to the protection gained. Forming an LLC in most states costs less than $500 in initial fees, and annual compliance costs typically run between $100 and $800 — a reasonable price to insulate your personal wealth from investment-related liability.
