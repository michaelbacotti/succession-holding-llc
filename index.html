Why Succession Planning Changes Everything

Real estate is one of the most tax-efficient ways to transfer wealth to the next generation. Through stepped-up basis rules, 1031 exchanges held until death, and charitable giving strategies, investors can dramatically reduce — or entirely eliminate — the estate tax burden on their children. The properties you've built don't have to become your heirs' problem.

A $500,000 property with a $50,000 cost basis passed to the next generation receives a full stepped-up basis. Capital gains taxes owed by the deceased are forgiven. The heir inherits at current market value — potentially saving six figures in taxes that would have been due if the property had changed hands through an outright sale.

We plan for our own family's transition. That means every strategy we write about, we implement for ourselves first. If something doesn't work in practice, we don't write about it. You'll never find a "10 secrets real estate agents don't want you to know" list on this site — because we don't write content we wouldn't use ourselves.

From the Field

Cap Rate vs. Cash-on-Cash Return

Cap rate measures value independent of financing. Cash-on-cash measures return on your actual equity invested. Most beginners chase cap rate. Sophisticated investors use both — and know when each matters.

The 1031 Identification Window

You have 45 days after closing to identify replacement properties — not 45 business days. Calendar days. Most investors who blow a 1031 exchange miss this detail. It's the most expensive calendar error in real estate investing.

Why We Prefer LLCs Over S-Corps for Real Estate

S-Corps save self-employment tax on earnings above a reasonable salary — but the cost to set up and maintain, plus the complexity of distributions, rarely makes sense for a portfolio under $1M in gross rentals. LLC with single-member taxation is simpler and equally effective.
