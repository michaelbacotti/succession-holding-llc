How to Appeal Your Property Tax Assessment (And Win)

Assessments are often wrong. Here is exactly how to challenge yours and win.

Last updated: April 2026 • By the Succession Holding LLC Research Team

Assessments are often wrong. Here is exactly how to challenge yours and win.

Property tax assessments are not gospel. They are estimates — sometimes accurate, sometimes wildly off — made by local government assessors working from limited data, compressed timelines, and imperfect methodology. The National Taxpayers Union estimates that 30% to 60% of residential properties are over-assessed to some degree. If you own real estate, there is a better-than-even chance your assessment is too high.

The good news: every state allows you to appeal. And appeals work. The Institute for Property Taxation reports that informal appeals succeed in reducing assessed value roughly 40% of the time when the homeowner presents credible comparable sales data. Formal appeals succeed at even higher rates when properly documented.

Understanding Your Tax Bill: Assessed Value vs. Market Value

Before you appeal, understand what you are looking at. Your property tax bill is based on the assessed value — not the market value. The relationship between the two varies by jurisdiction, but most states assess at some percentage of market value, typically 80% to 100%. A jurisdiction that assesses at 90% of market value will show an assessed value of $270,000 on a home that would sell for $300,000.

Your goal in an appeal is not to argue what the property is worth today — it is to argue what the assessed value should be based on recent arm's-length sales of comparable properties. These are two different arguments.

Research Comparable Sales

The foundation of any successful appeal is a comparable sales analysis. You need to find three to five properties that recently sold — within the past 12 to 18 months — that are similar to yours in:

  • Square footage and lot size
  • Number of bedrooms and bathrooms
  • Age and condition of any improvements
  • Location (same neighborhood or school district)
  • Zoning and land use

Your county assessor's website typically lists recent sales. You can also use public real estate listing records. For each comparable sale, record the sale price, date, and property characteristics. If your subject property has a feature the comparables lack — or lacks a feature the comparables have — note the adjustment you would make.

The standard rule of thumb: comparable properties should be within 0.5 miles and sold within 12 months for residential. For land, the radius expands and the time window tightens because raw land sales are less frequent.

Gathering Evidence: Appraisals, Comps, and Condition Documentation

A successful appeal requires more than a list of comparable sales. The most persuasive evidence packages combine multiple types of documentation that corroborate your valuation argument from different angles.

Professional Appraisal

A licensed real estate appraisal is the single most persuasive piece of evidence you can present. Appraisals cost $400 to $800 for a single-family home but carry significant weight with boards and assessors because they are performed by independent, licensed professionals using standardized methodology. The three approaches to value — sales comparison, cost approach, and income approach — are all documented in a professional report.

For rental properties, the income approach is particularly powerful: if the property's actual rental income produces a capitalization rate that implies a market value below the assessed value, that is a compelling and quantifiable argument. An investor who paid $200,000 for a property that generates $12,000/year in net operating income has evidence the market — not just sentiment — supports a lower value.

MLS Data and Listing History

Pull the MLS listing history for your property and comparable sales. If a property was listed for months at a price below its assessed value without attracting buyers, that is evidence the market does not support the assessment. Include expired listings at the assessed value as evidence of overvaluation — a property that cannot find a buyer at the assessor's implied price is strong evidence the assessment is too high.

Condition Documentation

Photos of deferred maintenance, structural issues, outdated mechanicals, or functional obsolescence support a lower assessed value. The assessor's field visit may have occurred when the property was in better condition, or the assessor may have missed visible deficiencies. A documented photo record — with timestamps — creates a contemporaneous record of the property's actual condition at the time of assessment.

For land properties, document encumbrances, access limitations, wetland flags, zoning restrictions, and environmental conditions that limit marketability. These facts may not appear in the assessor's database but materially affect value.

Assessor Records and Data Errors

Request the assessor's property record card for your property. This document shows exactly what data the assessor used — square footage, lot size, year built, bedroom/bath count, and any recorded improvements. Errors in this data (wrong square footage, wrong lot size, recorded improvements that were never built) are among the easiest grounds for appeal. A property recorded as 2,000 sq ft when it is actually 1,700 sq ft is a quantifiable overstatement that directly inflates the assessment.

Three Levels of Appeal

1. Informal Review (First Step)

Most jurisdictions start with an informal review. You submit a letter to the assessor's office contesting the value, accompanied by your comparable sales data. There is no hearing — a assessor reviewer simply reconsidered the file. This step is free and often effective. Do not skip it.

2. Board of Assessment Review or Board of Equalization (Formal)

If the informal review does not produce a satisfactory result, escalate to the local board. This is a quasi-judicial body that hears property tax disputes. You will typically submit a formal application and may be invited to present your case in person. Bring:

  • Your comparable sales grid
  • Photos of the property showing condition issues
  • Any recent appraisal you have commissioned
  • A written summary of your argument (three pages maximum)

3. State Tax Court or Circuit Court (Last Resort)

Most property tax disputes never reach this level. If yours does, you will benefit from an attorney specializing in property tax appeals. The cost-benefit threshold here is typically disputes involving properties valued over $500,000 where the assessment error exceeds $20,000.

Presenting Your Case to the Board of Revision

Formal hearings before the Board of Assessment Review are an underutilized opportunity. Most residential homeowners skip the hearing entirely, which is a mistake — the informal hearing is where many cases are won or lost on the basis of presentation quality alone.

Before the Hearing

Confirm the hearing format: some boards conduct paper reviews only, some hold in-person hearings, and some allow telephone or video appearances. Know your jurisdiction's format and prepare accordingly. Submit your evidence package at least one week before the hearing — boards that receive packets in advance can review materials before calling your name, making your presentation more efficient.

Prepare a one-page summary of your position. Lead with the number: "The assessed value is $X. Our analysis of five comparable sales shows market value of $Y, a gap of $Z." Boards review hundreds of cases; a clear, numerical opening statement anchors their attention.

At the Hearing

Be concise and professional. Present your comparable sales grid first — it is your strongest evidence. Walk the board through each comparable: address, sale price, date, square footage, and how it compares to your subject property. If your property has specific deficiencies relative to the comparables (smaller lot, older roof, no central air), acknowledge those and explain your adjustments.

If you have a professional appraisal, present it prominently. Appraisals from a licensed appraiser carry significant credibility. Mention the appraiser's credentials and the methodology used.

Answer questions directly. If the board asks why you think the value should be lower, point to your comps — do not rely on subjective statements about condition that the board may disagree with. Do not volunteer information that weakens your case, such as recent improvements you have made.

Do not attack the assessor personally or argue that the assessment is unfair in emotional terms. The board's job is to evaluate evidence, not adjudicate grievances. A professional, evidence-based presentation is far more persuasive than passion.

After the Hearing

Request a written decision regardless of outcome. If the board rules in your favor, confirm the new assessed value and verify it appears correctly on your next tax bill. If the ruling is adverse, ask for a clear explanation of the basis for the decision — this information is valuable for preparing a formal appeal to the next level.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

The most common reason appeals fail: homeowners argue market value in a market-value forum without understanding that they need to argue assessed value relative to comparable assessed values — not sale prices. Assessors and boards are looking for comparisons to other assessed values in the jurisdiction, not to what properties are selling for on the open market.

Other critical mistakes: missing filing deadlines (typically 30 to 60 days after the assessment notice is mailed — mark this date the day your notice arrives, not the postmark date); relying on a single comparable sale when most jurisdictions expect three to five; and failing to document physical deficiencies that justify a lower value. Appearing at a hearing unprepared — without a clear comp grid or data to support your position — signals to the board that you have not done the analytical work. Cases without credible supporting documentation are routinely denied.

Another common error: conflating market value arguments with assessed value arguments. Boards are not tasked with determining what your property would sell for today — they are tasked with evaluating whether the assessor's methodology produced an accurate assessed value. A strong appeal argues assessed value against comparable assessed values, not against current market sale prices.

Finally, do not ignore the assessment notice if you receive a reduction in the informal review. Reductions granted at the informal level are sometimes reversed or reduced further at the formal level — follow through to the final determination before concluding the process.

Timelines by State

Property tax appeal deadlines vary significantly. Texas homeowners have until May 31 or 30 days after the notice is mailed — whichever is later. New York requires filing within 30 days of the notice. California allows 60 days. Many states have different deadlines for unsecured personal property vs. real property.

Appeal deadlines by state — most common standards: Texas homeowners have until May 31 or 30 days after the notice is mailed, whichever is later. New York requires filing within 30 days of the notice. California allows 60 days from the notice date. Florida has a 25-day deadline for the Value Adjustment Board. Illinois allows 30 days post-mail. Ohio's deadline varies by county but is typically 30 days after the notice. Virginia allows 60 days. Georgia requires filing within 45 days of the notice.

Many states have completely different deadlines for personal property vs. real property, and commercial properties are often treated differently still. If you own both a primary residence and investment properties, verify the deadline separately for each. Note also that some jurisdictions have different deadlines for the informal vs. formal stages — the informal letter deadline may be earlier than the formal application deadline.

The safest approach: treat the appeal deadline as seven days after the notice date, not the deadline itself. This buffer accounts for mailing delays, administrative processing time, and any errors in your filing. File early whenever possible.

Find your state's specific deadlines at your state department of revenue website, or call the assessor's office and ask: "What is the deadline to file a property tax appeal?" Write it down. Missing the deadline means you pay the assessment and wait for the next cycle.

The Numbers Game

A $20,000 reduction in assessed value, in a jurisdiction that taxes at 1.5% of assessed value, saves you $300 per year in property taxes. Over a 10-year holding period, that is $3,000. A successful appeal on a $1 million commercial property that reduces assessed value by $100,000 saves $1,500 annually — $15,000 over a decade. The effort is the same whether you win $300 or $15,000. Pick your battles, but do not assume you cannot win.
