Landlord Essentials

The legal and practical foundations every landlord needs — from lease agreements to tenant rights, eviction procedures, and fair housing compliance.

Last updated: April 2026 • By the Succession Holding LLC Research Team

Managing rental property successfully requires more than finding a good tenant and collecting rent. Landlords operate within a framework of legal obligations, disclosure requirements, and procedural rules that vary by state and even by municipality. Understanding the essentials — and applying them consistently — is what separates professional landlords from those who expose themselves to unnecessary legal and financial risk.

Lease Agreements: Your Most Important Document

The lease agreement is the legal contract that governs the landlord-tenant relationship. It defines what is expected of each party, establishes the rules for the tenancy, and — most critically — provides the legal framework for enforcing those rules when things go wrong. A well-drafted lease is one of the most important risk management tools a landlord has.

An effective lease should clearly specify the property address, the names of all tenants, the lease term and renewal terms, the monthly rent amount and due date, the security deposit amount and terms, utilities responsibility, rules regarding pets and smoking, rules regarding alterations and modifications, the process for requesting maintenance and repairs, the landlord's access rights, the procedure for lease termination and notice periods, and any addenda such as pet addenda orMove-in inspection checklists.

Alaska landlords are subject to both state law and applicable municipal ordinances. Lease terms must comply with Alaska's residential landlord-tenant act, and any provision that attempts to waive rights provided by statute will be unenforceable. For example, a lease cannot legally prohibit a tenant from contacting a local housing authority or organizing with other tenants, even if the lease includes such a prohibition.

It is worth investing in a well-drafted lease written or reviewed by a real estate attorney familiar with Alaska law. The cost of professional lease drafting — typically a few hundred dollars — is far less than the cost of litigation over ambiguous lease terms.

Security Deposits: Rules and Limits

Security deposits serve as a financial buffer against tenant-caused damage, unpaid rent, and lease violations. Alaska law governs the handling of security deposits for residential properties, and landlords must follow these rules precisely or risk liability.

Alaska does not cap security deposit amounts at the state level, but many municipalities have their own rules. Landlords must clearly document the condition of the property at move-in using a written checklist signed by both landlord and tenant, with dated photographs or video. This move-in documentation is essential — without it, proving that damage occurred during the tenancy rather than before it is significantly more difficult.

Upon move-out, landlords have 14 days to return the security deposit along with an itemized list of any deductions — including amounts and descriptions of damage or unpaid rent. Failure to return the deposit and itemized statement within 14 days can expose the landlord to statutory damages of up to twice the amount of the deposit, plus the tenant's reasonable attorney fees. Even if a tenant owes more than the deposit covers, the landlord must follow the same procedure and document the full amount owed.

Security deposits must be kept separate from the landlord's personal operating funds — they cannot be comingled with business operating accounts or used for general purposes. Many landlords maintain a separate trust account for tenant security deposits.

Alaska Tenant Rights

Alaska tenants have statutory rights that landlords cannot waive through lease provisions. The most fundamental of these is the right to a fit and habitable dwelling. Landlords are required to maintain properties in a condition that meets applicable health and safety codes, keep structural components in good repair, provide running water and heat, and address pest infestations.

Tenants have the right to withhold rent — and in some cases, to terminate the lease — if a landlord fails to maintain the property in habitable condition after proper written notice. A tenant's remedy for uninhabitable conditions is not unlimited: the tenant must have given the landlord notice of the problem and allowed a reasonable time for repair, except in cases involving imminent danger to health or safety.

Tenants also have a right to privacy and cannot be subjected to unreasonable entry. Alaska law requires landlords to provide at least 24 hours' advance notice before entering an occupied unit for non-emergency purposes such as inspections or repairs. Landlords may enter without notice only in genuine emergencies such as a fire or a burst pipe.

Retaliation against tenants for exercising their legal rights is prohibited. If a tenant files a complaint with a housing authority or withholds rent for a legitimate habitability issue, a landlord cannot evict that tenant, raise the rent, or reduce services in response. Such retaliation is an independent cause of action in Alaska courts.

Eviction Process in Alaska

No landlord wants to evict a tenant, but understanding the process is essential for managing non-payment and lease violations efficiently. Eviction without proper legal process — including changing locks, removing doors, shutting off utilities, or removing tenant belongings — can expose a landlord to significant liability, including actual damages and statutory penalties of up to three months' rent.

The lawful eviction process in Alaska begins with a written notice to the tenant. For non-payment of rent, the notice must give the tenant five days to pay or vacate before the landlord can file an eviction lawsuit. For other lease violations, the notice period is typically ten days to cure or vacate. If the tenant does not comply, the landlord may then file an eviction action in the appropriate district court.

The court hearing typically occurs within a few weeks of filing, depending on the court's calendar. If the landlord prevails, the court issues a judgment for possession of the premises. A writ of assistance — the document that actually authorizes a law enforcement officer to physically remove the tenant — is issued after the judgment becomes final. The actual physical removal of a non-compliant tenant can take additional weeks after the judgment.

This timeline means that an eviction for non-payment of rent can take six to ten weeks from the initial notice to physical removal, assuming no delays. For this reason, landlords should screen tenants thoroughly before move-in — prevention is far less costly than the time and lost rental income of an eviction proceeding.

Fair Housing Laws

Fair housing laws prohibit discrimination in the rental of housing based on protected characteristics. The federal Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, and disability. Alaska state law adds two additional protected classes: ancestry and sexual orientation. Municipal codes may add further protections.

For landlords, the practical implications of fair housing compliance are significant. All marketing, tenant screening criteria, lease terms, and interactions with current and prospective tenants must be consistently applied regardless of any protected characteristic. A landlord cannot legally refuse to rent to families with children, refuse to rent to someone because of their religion or national origin, or impose different lease terms on tenants based on any protected trait.

The fair housing obligation applies not just to the initial rental decision but to ongoing property management — how repairs are prioritized, how lease violations are handled, and how tenant communications are managed. Even a single discriminatory statement made during the application process, if documented, can form the basis for a fair housing complaint or lawsuit.

Documented, consistent screening criteria — applied to every applicant in the same way — is the landlord's best protection against fair housing claims. Written criteria for evaluating tenant applications, maintained and applied uniformly, make it substantially easier to demonstrate that every rental decision was based on legitimate, non-discriminatory factors.
