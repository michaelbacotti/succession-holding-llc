Land Investing Guide

A comprehensive guide to evaluating, buying, financing, and holding raw land — with the due diligence framework used by experienced land investors.

Last updated: April 2026 • By the Succession Holding LLC Research Team

Raw land investing is one of the most straightforward paths into real estate — and one of the most frequently misunderstood. Unlike rental properties, land requires no tenants, no emergency repair calls, and no deferred maintenance reckoning. You buy it, you hold it, and eventually you sell it. That simplicity is the appeal. It is also precisely why the fundamentals matter so much: without the ongoing cash flow from rents to buffer mistakes, every error in acquisition, financing, or due diligence flows directly into your return calculation.

This guide covers everything you need to evaluate a land purchase, finance it responsibly, manage holding costs, and exit profitably. It is written for investors who are new to land — and for those who want to build a more disciplined approach to what can appear, deceptively, to be a simple asset class.

Why Invest in Land?

Before evaluating any specific parcel, it is worth being clear about why land belongs in an investment portfolio at all — and why it may or may not be appropriate for your specific situation.

Lower Entry Cost

Raw land is often priced significantly below comparable developed property. Parcels in rural areas can be purchased for a few thousand dollars, making land one of the most accessible real estate investment categories. This low entry cost is meaningful: it allows investors to build a diversified position across multiple parcels and markets without the capital requirements of improved property.

Minimal Management

Land does not leak, does not have tenants who stop paying, and does not require utility connections. Between purchase and sale, your primary responsibilities are paying property taxes and monitoring for encroachments or zoning changes. For investors who want real estate exposure without the operational demands of being a landlord, land is an attractive option.

Portfolio Diversification

Land values do not move in lockstep with housing markets. A parcel in a rural county is affected by different supply and demand dynamics than a city condominium — which can be a portfolio advantage during economic downturns. Land also tends to have lower correlation with traditional financial assets, making it useful for investors seeking true diversification.

Development Upside

Land near growing communities often appreciates as infrastructure arrives. A parcel five miles outside a city's current boundary may sit within city limits within a decade — dramatically changing its value and the pool of potential buyers. Investors who can identify infrastructure trends early can acquire land at prices that do not yet reflect the coming change.

The Illiquidity Reality

Land is significantly less liquid than developed property. A retail buyer for a single-family home exists in virtually every market; a retail buyer for a 40-acre rural parcel in a specific county may take years to materialize. Investors must be comfortable with capital being locked up for extended periods — and must plan holding costs accordingly.

Types of Land: Finding the Right Category

Not all land is the same investment. The type of land you buy determines your buyer pool, your holding costs, your financing options, and your timeline to exit. Understanding these categories — and matching them to your investment thesis — is the first major decision in land investing.

Rural and Recreational Land

Rural recreational parcels are often the first land investment for beginners. These include wooded acreage, hunting land, fishing properties, and general recreation parcels. Buyer profiles vary widely: outdoor enthusiasts seeking a personal retreat, hunters and anglers, weekend campers, and investors who see long-term value in rural acreage near population centers.

Rural land is typically priced affordably and carries low carrying costs relative to its value. The downside is a thin buyer pool and extended time on market when it comes time to sell. Investors in recreational land should be patient and should budget for multi-year holding periods.

Residential Development Land

Land zoned for residential development — either single-family lots or multi-family parcels — sits closer to the developed property value chain. This type of land is typically purchased by builders or developers who intend to construct homes. The value of a residential development parcel is a function of the finished lot value minus the cost to develop: entitlements, utility connections, road extension, grading, and financing.

Residential development land requires more due diligence than recreational parcels — zoning confirmation, utility feasibility studies, and a clear understanding of local development fees are essential. The upside potential is also higher, particularly in markets with limited developable land supply and growing populations.

Commercial Land

Commercially-zoned land is intended for retail, office, hospitality, or industrial development. Commercial land transactions are larger, more complex, and involve longer due diligence cycles than residential land deals. The buyer pool for commercial land is primarily professional developers and business owners seeking a location for their operations.

Commercial land investing generally requires more capital, more expertise, and longer holding periods than other land categories. It is best suited for experienced investors with established networks in commercial real estate.

Agricultural Land

Agricultural land is valued based on its productive capacity — income per acre from crops, livestock, or timber. Investors in agricultural land may be seeking current income from leases to farmers, long-term appreciation as the land is absorbed into development, or both.

Agricultural land in many states receives favorable property tax treatment under current-use assessment programs — which reduces carrying costs but typically requires the owner to demonstrate active agricultural use. Breaking this preferential assessment (through development or sale to a non-agricultural buyer) can trigger additional taxes, sometimes called a rollback tax.

Timberland

Timberland is a specialized category where the primary value driver is standing timber inventory. Investors in timberland may hold the land for timber harvest revenue, for long-term land appreciation, or for a combination of both. Timberland can also generate income through recreational leases — hunting leases on timbered property are common in the Southeast and Pacific Northwest.

The Due Diligence Framework for Land

Due diligence for land is different from residential property due diligence — there is no home inspector to call, no appraisal contingencies to navigate. But land due diligence is no less important, and the mistakes are permanent. A parcel purchased without proper title verification or access confirmation can become an unsellable liability.

Title and Ownership Verification

Before you buy any land, order a full title search and review the title commitment carefully. The title commitment is a preliminary document from a title company that identifies all issues that must be resolved before the title company will insure the property. Look specifically for:

  • Liens and encumbrances — unpaid mortgages, tax liens, mechanic's liens, or judgments against prior owners can survive a sale and become your problem
  • Easements — utility easements, access easements, and drainage easements run with the land and constrain what you can do with the property; understand each one
  • Deed restrictions and covenants — private restrictions on land use, building size, or aesthetic standards can limit your options significantly
  • Chain of title gaps — missing deeds, unclear conveyances, or family estate issues in the chain of title can create clouds on title that are expensive to resolve

Title insurance is available for land purchases and is generally inexpensive relative to the property value. Do not skip it.

Legal Access: The Non-Negotiable

A parcel without legal access to a public road — commonly called "landlocked" property — is extremely difficult to sell, difficult to develop, and difficult to finance. Before closing on any land purchase, verify that the property has documented legal access: either frontage on a dedicated public road, or a recorded easement granting access rights to a neighboring parcel.

Be cautious of easements that are informal, undocumented, or based on neighborly arrangements rather than recorded legal instruments. An easement that is not in the public record may not transfer to a future buyer, and a future landowner may not honor an undocumented arrangement.

Zoning and Permitted Uses

Check the current zoning classification with the county or municipal planning department and confirm what uses are permitted by right, by conditional use permit, and what uses are prohibited. A parcel zoned agricultural-residential supports a different buyer pool than one zoned commercial or light industrial — and those differences dramatically affect value.

Also inquire about any pending rezoning proposals in the area. A county's future land use map, if publicly available, will show planned zoning designations for the coming years. A parcel currently zoned agricultural but designated for future residential development may be worth significantly more than its current use suggests.

Utilities and Infrastructure

For land you intend to develop or sell to someone who will build on it, utility availability is a critical value factor. Key questions:

  • Is the parcel within reach of municipal water and sewer, or will a well and septic system be required?
  • Is electrical service available at the parcel boundary, or is extension required?
  • What is the distance to the nearest natural gas line, if applicable?

The cost to extend utilities to a parcel can be substantial — $10,000 to $50,000 or more for a rural extension — and this cost affects both your development budget and the price a future builder can pay for the lot. Always factor in these infrastructure costs when evaluating raw land value.

Soil, Topography, and Environmental

USDA Web Soil Survey maps are a free resource that provide soil classifications for virtually any parcel in the United States. Soil type affects both agricultural productivity and development feasibility — certain soil types are unsuitable for septic systems, for example, or have high shrink-swell potential that affects foundation construction.

Check FEMA flood zone designation (available free at the FEMA Flood Map Service Center). Parcels in Special Flood Hazard Areas will require flood insurance if financed, and may be undevelopable in certain configurations. Also check for any registered wetlands that could restrict development — the Army Corps of Engineers has jurisdiction over filling wetlands, and wetland violations carry significant penalties.

Water Rights

In many western states, water rights are separate from land ownership and can be more valuable than the land itself. Before buying in states like Colorado, Utah, Idaho, or Montana, determine whether the parcel includes groundwater rights, whether a well permit is available from the state engineer, and whether the property is in a designated groundwater management district. In eastern states, water rights are generally riparian — attached to land bordering water bodies — but the specifics vary.

Financing Land Deals

Financing raw land is materially different from financing a house. Most traditional lenders — banks and mortgage companies — view land loans as higher risk than improved property loans. Interest rates are higher, down payment requirements are larger, and loan terms are shorter. Understanding your financing options before you make an offer is essential.

Seller Financing

Many land sellers — particularly private landowners with acreage — are open to seller financing arrangements. The mechanics are straightforward: you make a down payment directly to the seller and execute a promissory note for the balance, with the land serving as collateral via a deed of trust or mortgage. Terms vary widely, but typical seller-financed land deals include 10% to 30% down payments, interest rates between 5% and 10%, and amortization schedules of 5 to 15 years.

Seller financing eliminates bank appraisal requirements and lender delays — a significant advantage in competitive land markets. Many sellers prefer the steady income stream and are motivated to offer favorable terms to close. Be sure to document the arrangement with a properly executed promissory note and deed of trust recorded in the county land records.

Cash Purchases

Cash purchases are the most straightforward and often the most negotiator-friendly. When you pay cash, you eliminate financing contingencies, appraisal requirements, and lender delays — making your offer more attractive to sellers who want a clean, fast transaction. Land parcels frequently sell at meaningful discounts to cash buyers, particularly in rural markets where financed buyers face longer timelines and higher costs.

If you do not have sufficient cash reserves, consider partnering with another investor or using a line of credit against existing assets rather than using traditional financing, which can be difficult to obtain for raw land.

Local Banks and Credit Unions

Community banks and credit unions with local market knowledge are often more willing to originate land loans than large national lenders. Because they understand regional land values and development patterns, they can underwrite land loans with more nuance than automated underwriting systems. Establish a relationship with a local lender before you need financing — and be prepared to present a clear exit strategy explaining how you intend to sell the parcel and repay the loan.

Specialized Land Lenders

A number of lenders specialize in raw land financing, including some hard money lenders and land-focused credit institutions. These lenders understand the longer hold periods typical of land investing and structure products accordingly — though interest rates are materially higher than conventional mortgage products. Use specialized land lenders as a bridge or a tool for specific situations, not as a primary financing strategy.

Holding Costs You Must Budget For

The cost of holding land between purchase and sale is one of the most underestimated expenses in land investing. Unlike rental property, where rental income offsets carrying costs, land holding costs are pure overhead. Factor these into your analysis before you buy.

Property Taxes

Land is taxed annually at the applicable mill levy for its classification. In many states, agricultural or forested parcels receive preferential current-use assessment — reducing property taxes in exchange for maintaining qualifying use. However, if you change the use or sell to a non-qualifying buyer, a rollback tax may apply, adding several years of the tax savings back to your bill. Budget for the full tax burden, not just the current-use amount.

Land Stewardship

Depending on the parcel and its location, you may need periodic brush hogging, fence maintenance, weed control, or general upkeep to keep the property marketable and compliant with local ordinances. Budget $300 to $1,500 annually for basic stewardship, more if the parcel is larger or has significant vegetation management needs.

Vacant Land Insurance

Vacant land insurance is relatively inexpensive but not free. A basic liability policy costs roughly $300 to $600 per year for most rural parcels. This coverage protects you if someone is injured on the property — without it, you are personally exposed to liability claims. Some landowners forgo this coverage on the assumption that no one ever visits — this is a calculated risk that rarely ends well.

Opportunity Cost

Capital tied up in land is capital not earning returns elsewhere. This is not a cash outlay, but it belongs in your investment analysis. A parcel purchased for $25,000 that sits for seven years before selling for $40,000 generated a nominal gain — but if that capital could have earned 8% annually in an alternative investment, the true economic return was substantially lower than it appears.

Exit Strategies for Land Investors

Successful land investors plan their exit before they buy. Understanding your target buyer and your timeline shapes everything from which parcels you purchase to how you hold title.

Retail Resale

The most straightforward exit: buy wholesale or at a discount, make modest improvements — clearing, grading, a gravel drive, a property boundary survey — and resell to a retail buyer. Retail buyers include future homeowners seeking a building site, small farmers or ranchers, outdoor recreation enthusiasts, and investors building a land position.

Retail sales generate the highest margins but take longer and require more marketing effort. Listing on Zillow, LandWatch, and local MLS platforms reaches the broadest retail buyer pool.

Wholesale to Other Investors

Some investors specialize in finding discounted land and reselling — not to end users, but to other land investors at a markup. This wholesale approach turns inventory faster and requires less capital for improvements, but margins per transaction are smaller. Building relationships with other land investors in your target markets is the key to executing this strategy consistently.

Lease-Option to Builders

A builder or developer may want to control a parcel now but cannot close for 12 to 24 months while they complete rezoning or plat approval. A lease-option agreement lets you collect monthly payments during the hold period with the option fee and purchase price locked in today. This strategy generates income during the hold period and reduces your exposure to market softening.

Hunting and Recreational Leases

Rural land with timber, wildlife, or water features can generate meaningful income through recreational leases. A one-year exclusive hunting lease on a wooded parcel in the Midwest or Southeast can produce $1,000 to $5,000 or more annually — enough to offset property taxes and carrying costs while you wait for the right buyer. Wildlife management lease arrangements in Texas and other states can generate even higher income per acre.

Common Land Investor Mistakes to Avoid

Land investing has a higher proportion of retail participants who lose money than most other real estate categories — primarily because it appears simple and attracts investors who have not done their homework. These are the most common mistakes.

Buying Without a Verified Exit

The single most common land investing mistake is buying a parcel without a clear idea of who the eventual buyer will be. If you cannot identify a plausible path to liquidity — a specific type of buyer, a specific use case, a realistic timeline — you may be buying a position that cannot be exited profitably. Before you buy, ask yourself: who will want this parcel, why will they want it, and how will they find it?

Overpaying at Acquisition

Retail prices for land are not the same as investment-grade acquisition prices. The most important number in a land investment is your acquisition cost. A parcel purchased at retail pricing can take a decade or more to appreciate enough to generate a positive return after holding costs. Buy at wholesale — from motivated sellers, at a discount to comparable retail value — and the math improves dramatically.

Ignoring Holding Costs

Buyers who calculate returns based on purchase price and expected sale price without accounting for annual property taxes, insurance, stewardship, and opportunity cost frequently discover that their "profitable" land deal generated a negative return in real terms. Model all carrying costs into your investment analysis before you close.

Not Verifying Access

Buying landlocked property — or property where the access arrangement is informal, undocumented, or subject to dispute — is a mistake that is difficult to recover from. Always verify legal access before closing, and never rely on verbal assurances from sellers or neighbors.

Assuming No Title Issues

Title problems do not announce themselves. A missing deed in the chain of title, an undisclosed easement, or a lien from a previous owner can turn a straightforward land deal into years of legal expenses. Always order a title commitment and review it carefully before closing.

Failing to Account for Zoning Uncertainty

A parcel that looks buildable may have zoning, soil, or access constraints that prevent development. Confirm all material facts with the county planning department directly — do not rely on what the seller or a listing agent tells you. A 30-minute call with the planning department can prevent a costly mistake.

The Land Investor's Due Diligence Checklist

Before closing on any land purchase, complete the following:

  • Order a title search and review the title commitment for exceptions, liens, and encumbrances
  • Purchase title insurance
  • Verify property boundaries with a licensed surveyor or confirm against existing plat maps
  • Confirm property tax status — no delinquent taxes, understand the current tax burden
  • Review all existing easements and assess their impact on intended use
  • Confirm zoning classification and permitted uses with the local planning department
  • Inquire about pending annexations, rezoning proposals, and large-scale development projects nearby
  • Check for special assessments (road districts, drainage districts, municipal utility districts)
  • Review USDA soil maps and any available perc test results
  • Verify legal access to a public road or existence of a recorded easement
  • Check FEMA flood zone designation
  • Confirm availability of groundwater rights and well permit availability (where applicable)
  • Investigate wetlands designations and any Army Corps of Engineers requirements

Summary

Land investing rewards patience, thorough due diligence, and a clear understanding of local market dynamics. The investors who do well with land are the ones who study a market thoroughly before buying — who know the zoning codes, the development pipeline, the water table depth, and the likely profile of the eventual buyer. They also buy at the right price, account for all carrying costs, and plan their exit before they close.

The most important rule: never pay retail. Land sells at its greatest discounts to buyers who can close quickly, pay cash, and make decisions without committee approval. Build those capabilities — or build relationships with investors who have them — and the deals will come.
