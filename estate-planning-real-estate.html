Estate Planning for Real Estate Investors

Structure your holdings so they transfer cleanly — and tax-efficiently — to the next generation.

Last updated: April 2026 • By the Succession Holding LLC Research Team

Real estate is the largest asset class for most American families — and the most complicated to transfer at death. Unlike a brokerage account, where beneficiary designations determine the outcome, real estate passes through a tangle of title documents, wills, and state laws. Without proper planning, the property your family spent decades accumulating can become a months-long probate nightmare with tens of thousands of dollars in legal fees.

This guide walks through how sophisticated real estate investors think about succession planning — the structures they use, the tax implications they manage, and the practical steps that make the difference between a clean transfer and a family conflict.

Why Real Estate Complicates Estate Planning

A primary residence rarely presents significant estate planning challenges — it passes to a surviving spouse through joint tenancy with right of survivorship, or through a will to designated beneficiaries. The complexity arises when you hold multiple investment properties, when properties are in different states, when there are multiple heirs with conflicting interests, or when the estate approaches federal exemption thresholds.

Consider a common scenario: an investor owns three rental properties, one in her personal name and two in separate LLCs. She has a will but no trusts. Upon her death, the property in her personal name goes through probate — a court-supervised process averaging 6 to 18 months in most states. The LLC-owned properties are governed by the operating agreements, which may or may not have adequate succession provisions. The result: a fractured estate, conflicting heir expectations, and legal fees that erode the generational wealth she worked a lifetime to build.

The solution is not complicated — but it does require intentional structure built before it is needed.

How Investors Structure Their Holdings

There are three primary ways to hold real estate for investment purposes, each with distinct implications for estate planning, liability protection, and tax management.

Individual Ownership

Simplest in concept — the investor holds title directly, in their own name or as a joint tenant. This approach involves minimal setup cost and straightforward tax reporting (Schedule E on Form 1040). However, it provides no liability separation between the rental activity and the owner's personal assets, and it does not facilitate clean succession. Individual ownership is appropriate for small portfolios with modest values and where the owner has simple family circumstances — but it scales poorly for serious investors.

LLC Ownership

A Limited Liability Company holding rental real estate is the most common structure for serious real estate investors. The LLC provides liability separation: any lawsuit related to the rental property is generally limited to the LLC's assets, not the member's personal assets. From an estate planning perspective, the LLC is a transferable interest — it can be gifted, sold, or bequeathed as a whole, rather than requiring the transfer of individual properties one at a time.

A well-drafted LLC operating agreement should include clear provisions for what happens upon the death, disability, or withdrawal of a member. Without these provisions, the default state LLC statutes govern — and the defaults rarely match what the investor intended. Key provisions should address whether heirs become members automatically, whether remaining members have the right to purchase the deceased member's interest, and what valuation method applies to that purchase option.

For investors with multiple properties, holding each property (or group of similar properties) in its own LLC adds another layer of protection. A lawsuit at one property stays contained within that LLC rather than threatening the entire portfolio.

Trust Ownership

Revocable living trusts are a popular estate planning vehicle precisely because they allow property to pass outside of probate — directly to named beneficiaries upon the death of the trustee, without court involvement. For primary residences and even investment properties, a revocable trust can simplify succession significantly.

Irrevocable trusts, by contrast, remove assets permanently from the grantor's estate — which can be advantageous for estate tax planning but sacrifices flexibility and control. Some investors use irrevocable trusts as part of a broader estate reduction strategy, particularly when the estate approaches federal exemption limits.

The tradeoff with trusts is complexity and cost. A properly drafted trust requires legal fees, ongoing administration, and (for irrevocable trusts) a loss of control over the assets inside. For most individual investors with portfolios under $5 million, a combination of LLCs and a simple will provides 80% of the benefit at 20% of the cost.

Estate Tax Implications for Rental Property

The federal estate tax currently applies to taxable estates exceeding $13.61 million per individual in 2026, with a married couple effectively able to shield $27.22 million through portability provisions. Estates above these thresholds face a 40% tax rate on the excess — a significant一笔 that can force the sale of illiquid real estate assets to pay tax bills.

For investors whose estates approach or exceed the exemption, several strategies are relevant:

The Stepped-Up Basis Advantage

One of the most significant tax benefits of inherited real estate is the stepped-up basis rule: inherited property receives a new cost basis equal to its fair market value on the date of the owner's death. If an investor buys a rental property for $200,000 and it is worth $500,000 when they die, the heir inherits it with a basis of $500,000 — not $200,000. If the heir sells immediately for $500,000, there is no capital gains tax owed.

This benefit can save heirs hundreds of thousands of dollars in taxes. However, Congress has periodically proposed limiting stepped-up basis as part of broader tax reform. Investors with large portfolios should not rely on stepped-up basis as their sole estate planning strategy — it should be one component of a more comprehensive plan.

Annual Gift Tax Exclusion

The annual gift tax exclusion allows taxpayers to gift up to $19,000 per recipient (2026) without using any of their lifetime exemption. Investors can use this to gradually transfer LLC membership interests or real property to heirs over time, reducing the taxable estate while retaining some control during the transfer period.

Qualified Opportunity Zone Investments

For investors who purchase property in designated Opportunity Zones, there are specific estate tax advantages that can reduce estate tax exposure for gains invested in QOZ property. This is a more complex strategy that requires careful planning but may be worth exploring for investors with significant capital gains.

Succession Planning for Real Estate Portfolios

Succession planning for a real estate portfolio is not a single document — it is a set of interlocking decisions about how ownership, management, and control will pass from one generation to the next.

Identifying Successors Early

The first step is identifying who will manage and own the portfolio after you. This conversation is harder than it sounds — family dynamics, differing levels of interest in real estate, and unequal contributions to the portfolio over the years all create complexity. The best time to have this conversation is before there is urgency — when you can think clearly and your heirs can ask questions without the emotional weight of recent loss.

Building Management Capacity

An inherited portfolio is only as valuable as the ability of the next generation to manage it. A collection of rental properties with no one who understands landlord-tenant law, maintenance coordination, or financing can quickly become a liability. Investors who intend to pass their portfolio to family members should actively build that capacity — involving heirs in decisions, explaining the reasoning behind structural choices, and gradually delegating management responsibilities.

The Role of a Holding Company

A holding company structure — where a parent entity (often an LLC) sits above individual property LLCs — is one of the most effective succession planning tools available to real estate investors. Instead of passing individual properties to heirs one at a time, you pass the holding company's membership interests. One transfer document covers the entire portfolio. This dramatically reduces legal complexity, cost, and the risk of partial distributions that leave some heirs with mismatched stakes.

Succession Holding LLC, for example, was designed with this principle in mind — creating a central entity that holds ownership stakes in subsidiary entities, each managing specific properties. This structure allows the entire portfolio to be managed, valued, and transferred as a coherent whole.

Transfer-on-Death Deeds

An increasing number of states — 27 as of 2026, plus the District of Columbia — allow real property to be transferred via Transfer-on-Death (TOD) deed, sometimes called a Beneficiary Deed. A TOD deed transfers the property directly to a named beneficiary upon the owner's death, entirely outside of probate.

The owner retains full control of the property during life: they can sell, mortgage, or otherwise encumber it without the beneficiary's consent. The TOD designation can be changed or revoked at any time. This makes TOD deeds a flexible, low-cost succession tool for straightforward property holdings.

TOD deeds are less appropriate for properties held in LLCs or when the estate includes complex tax planning strategies that require trust structures. They are also not available in every state — investors should confirm availability in their state before relying on this mechanism.

Alaska-Specific Considerations

Alaska's unique legal environment affects estate planning for real estate in several ways. Alaska has no state income tax and no state estate tax — making it one of the most favorable jurisdictions for high-value real estate holdings from a tax perspective. Alaska also recognizes TOD deeds, giving investors an additional succession tool beyond what is available in some states.

For properties in Alaska's rural communities, succession planning faces additional practical challenges. Many rural Alaska properties are accessible only seasonally, have limited comparables for valuation purposes, and may be subject to Alaska Native corporation land entitlements or other unique title issues. Investors with Alaska holdings should work with an Alaska-licensed real estate attorney familiar with these local conditions.

The Alaska Landlord-Tenant Act governs residential rental relationships in Alaska, and any succession plan for Alaska rental property should account for how tenancy transfers upon the owner's death — including the rights of surviving family members who may occupy the property and the obligations of the estate regarding existing lease agreements.

Minimum Viable Estate Plan for Real Estate Investors

Every real estate investor — regardless of portfolio size — should have at minimum:

  • A will — designates how property held in your individual name is distributed; without a will, state intestacy laws determine who inherits, and it may not be who you would choose
  • LLC operating agreements with succession provisions — for each LLC holding rental property; these should be reviewed by an attorney and updated every 3–5 years
  • Healthcare directive and power of attorney — allows someone you trust to make medical and financial decisions if you become incapacitated; without these, a court-appointed guardian may be necessary at significant cost
  • Beneficiary designations reviewed annually — for any TOD accounts, IRAs, 401(k)s, and life insurance policies; beneficiary designations override what your will says

Online will services are appropriate for people with simple situations and modest assets. Real estate investors with multiple properties, LLCs, and multi-state holdings need an estate planning attorney — typically charging $2,000 to $5,000 for a comprehensive estate plan. That investment buys a plan that can save ten times that amount in probate fees, tax liability, and family conflict.

Review and Update Regularly

An estate plan is not a document you create once and forget. It should be reviewed every three to five years, or after any major life event: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, purchase or sale of significant assets, or a substantial change in federal or state tax law. The sunset of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions in 2026 makes this an especially important time for investors with substantial real estate holdings to review their succession plans with a qualified estate planning attorney.

The goal of all this planning is straightforward: to ensure that the wealth and property you have built over your lifetime transfers to the next generation on your terms, with minimal friction, cost, and family conflict.
