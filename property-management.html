Property Management

A practical guide to managing rental properties effectively — from tenant screening to ongoing maintenance and compliance.

Last updated: April 2026 • By the Succession Holding LLC Research Team

Owning rental properties can be a rewarding long-term investment strategy, but it comes with a set of ongoing responsibilities that go far beyond the initial purchase. Effective property management is what separates a profitable, stress-free investment from a costly, time-consuming liability. Whether you manage properties yourself or work with a professional property manager, understanding the fundamentals will help you make better decisions and protect your investment.

Tenant Screening: Your First Line of Defense

The single most important risk management decision you will make as a landlord is who you allow to rent your property. A bad tenant can cost thousands of dollars in missed rent, property damage, legal fees, and extended vacancies. A thorough, consistent screening process is the foundation of successful property management.

A complete screening package should include:

  • Credit Report — A credit report reveals how a prospective tenant handles financial obligations. Look for a reasonable credit score and watch specifically for previous evictions, unpaid collections, or bankruptcies.
  • Criminal Background Check — Felony convictions relevant to property damage or safety may be disqualifying. Minor offenses from years ago should be evaluated in context.
  • Employment and Income Verification — Confirm that the prospective tenant has stable, verifiable income. A common rule of thumb is that rent should not exceed 30% of gross monthly income.
  • Rental History — Contact previous landlords directly when possible. Ask about payment history, property condition at move-in and move-out, and whether they would rent to this tenant again.
  • Reference Checks — Personal and professional references provide additional insight into a tenant's reliability, though they should supplement — not replace — the above checks.

Apply the same screening criteria to every applicant. Using different standards for different applicants creates legal exposure under fair housing laws. Document your screening process and the reasons for every approval or denial.

Maintenance: Protecting Your Asset Over Time

Rental properties require ongoing maintenance to preserve their value, attract quality tenants, and avoid costly emergency repairs. A property that is not maintained deteriorates quickly — deferred maintenance compounds, turning small problems into large and expensive ones.

Preventive Maintenance is always preferable to reactive repair. Schedule regular inspections — at minimum annually, and ideally at each lease renewal. Inspect heating and cooling systems, plumbing, roofing, gutters, exterior siding, and appliances. Catching a minor roof leak in the fall can prevent thousands of dollars in water damage during winter.

Responding to Tenant Requests requires a balanced approach. Tenants have a legal right to a habitable property, and unresponsive landlords face legal liability. That said, not every maintenance request is an emergency. Establish clear expectations in the lease about what constitutes an emergency versus a routine request, and provide multiple contact channels for tenants to submit maintenance requests.

Building a Maintenance Reserve is essential. A commonly cited guideline is to set aside 1–2% of the property's value each year for maintenance and repairs. This fund provides a financial cushion so that unexpected repairs — a failed water heater, a broken furnace, storm damage — do not create cash flow crises.

Keep detailed records of all maintenance activity, including dates, descriptions, costs, and contractor information. These records are valuable for tax purposes, resale negotiations, and demonstrating that the property has been well-maintained.

Evictions: Understanding the Process

No landlord wants to evict a tenant. Yet every landlord will eventually face a situation where it becomes necessary. Understanding the eviction process before you need it — and taking preventive steps to reduce the likelihood — is a critical part of property management.

The best eviction is the one you prevent. Rigorous tenant screening is the most effective prevention tool. A tenant who was thoroughly vetted, has stable income, and signed a lease that clearly outlines their obligations is far less likely to create an eviction situation than one who was approved hastily.

When an eviction becomes necessary, the process varies by state but generally follows a consistent sequence. The landlord first provides written notice to the tenant — often a Pay or Quit notice specifying a deadline. If the tenant does not comply or vacate, the landlord files an eviction lawsuit (unlawful detainer action) with the local court. A court hearing follows, and if the landlord prevails, a writ of possession is issued, allowing the sheriff or constable to physically remove the tenant if they still refuse to leave.

The entire process can take anywhere from a few weeks in landlord-friendly jurisdictions to several months in more tenant-protective areas. During this time, the property is typically not generating rental income. This is why legal counsel familiar with local landlord-tenant law is an important resource — and why preventive screening is so valuable.

Self-help evictions are illegal in every state. Locking a tenant out, shutting off utilities, or removing their belongings without following the legal process exposes the landlord to significant legal liability, including potential counterclaims from the tenant.

Insurance: Protecting Against Major Losses

Rental property insurance — often called landlord insurance or dwelling fire insurance — is fundamentally different from a homeowner's primary residence policy. It is specifically designed to cover the risks associated with renting out a property to tenants.

A standard rental property policy typically covers:

  • Property Damage — Coverage for the structure (not the tenant's belongings) in cases of fire, wind, vandalism, and other covered perils.
  • Loss of Rental Income — If a covered loss renders the property uninhabitable, this coverage compensates for lost rental income during the repair period.
  • Liability Protection — If a tenant or visitor is injured on the property and the landlord is found negligent, liability coverage pays for legal defense and any resulting judgments.

Landlord insurance does not cover the tenant's personal belongings — that is the tenant's responsibility, typically through a renter's insurance policy. Many landlords now require tenants to carry renter's insurance as a lease condition, which is an inexpensive way to reduce disputes when tenant belongings are damaged or stolen.

Review your policy annually with your insurance agent. As properties age, rebuilding costs change, and your portfolio grows, your coverage needs will evolve. Adequate liability limits are particularly important — one lawsuit from an injured visitor can threaten an investor's entire financial position if liability coverage is insufficient.

Working with a Property Manager

For investors who own multiple properties, live far from their rental properties, or simply prefer not to manage day-to-day landlord responsibilities, a professional property management company can be an excellent solution. Property managers handle the full range of landlord duties — from marketing vacant units and screening tenants to collecting rent, coordinating maintenance, and managing legal compliance.

The cost of professional management typically ranges from 8–12% of monthly rental income, plus additional fees for lease renewals, placement (finding a new tenant), and maintenance coordination. For some investors, these fees are worth every dollar — particularly if the alternative is a long-distance commute to handle tenant emergencies or a lost weekend managing routine requests.

What to look for in a property manager:

  • Local Market Knowledge — A manager who understands the local rental market, comparable rents, and local tenant expectations will position your property competitively.
  • Clear Communication — Responsive communication is essential. Ask about their typical response times for tenant requests and how they communicate with owners.
  • Transparent Reporting — Monthly financial statements, maintenance logs, and open communications about property conditions are standard expectations.
  • Proper Licensing — Most states require property managers to hold a real estate broker's or property manager's license. Verify credentials before signing a management agreement.
  • Proven Process — Ask about their tenant screening criteria, lease templates, rent collection procedures, and maintenance dispatch protocols. Consistency and documentation protect both you and the tenant.

The relationship between an investor and a property manager is a partnership. A good property manager will proactively flag maintenance issues before they become emergencies, identify opportunities to increase rental income at lease renewal, and handle the day-to-day complexity so the investor can focus on portfolio strategy and growth.
